# Encounters

When the party meets creatures whose presence was not anticipated — a wandering monster, a road patrol, a band of refugees — the **encounter procedure** determines how the meeting begins and how it resolves. Encounters are triggered by [dungeon](dungeon-exploration.md) or [wilderness](wilderness-travel.md) checks, or scripted by the referee.

## Encounter Sequence

::: {.callout-important title="Encounter Sequence"}
When an encounter occurs, work through these steps in order:

1. **Distance** — determine how far apart the two sides start.
2. **Surprise** — check whether either side is caught unaware.
3. **Reaction** — if the encounter is not immediate combat, determine the creature's disposition.
4. **Resolve** — negotiate, flee, fight, or part ways. If combat begins, follow the [Combat Sequence](combat.md#the-combat-round).
:::

## Encounter Distance

The referee may set encounter distance from the situation, or roll randomly:

| Environment                | Distance         |
| -------------------------- | ---------------- |
| Dungeon                    | 2d6 × 10'        |
| Wilderness                 | 4d6 × 10 yards   |
| Wilderness, surprised side | 1d4 × 10 yards   |

Halve the distance in fog, dense forest, darkness, or other low-visibility conditions.

## Surprise

::: {.callout-important title="Surprise"}
Each side that could be caught unaware rolls 1d6. A side **surprises on a 1–2**.

A surprised side loses its action in the first round; the non-surprised side acts freely before initiative begins. If both sides are surprised, the confusion cancels and initiative is rolled normally.
:::

A party carrying a light in a dark environment usually **cannot surprise** others in that darkness, though they may still be surprised by creatures hidden beyond their light radius.

## Reactions

When the party meets a creature or group whose disposition is uncertain, the referee rolls to determine how it responds.

::: {.callout-important title="Reaction Roll"}
Roll **2d6 + CHA modifier** of the character leading the interaction.

| 2d6 Result | Reaction                                                    |
| ---------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| 2 or less  | **Hostile** — attacks or flees in panic                     |
| 3–5        | **Unfriendly** — suspicious; may attack if provoked         |
| 6–8        | **Neutral** — cautious; will negotiate or wait              |
| 9–11       | **Friendly** — open to interaction; may help if asked       |
| 12+        | **Enthusiastic** — actively helpful                         |
:::

The referee may apply ±1 for situation, gifts, or shared language.

A reaction is not a social override — a Friendly dragon still expects tribute, and a Neutral bandit may stand down only when outmatched.

## Evasion and Pursuit

A side that wishes to avoid combat may attempt to **evade** before initiative is rolled. Evasion is only possible if the encounter has not already escalated to violence.

### Dungeon Evasion

Compare combat movement rates. If the fleeing side is **faster**, evasion succeeds automatically. If pursuers are equal or faster, the fleeing side has a **2-in-6 chance** per round of breaking line of sight and escaping. Most creatures cease pursuit when sight is lost; intelligent pursuers may follow further at the referee's discretion.

### Wilderness Evasion

Compare overland movement rates and roll once per day of pursuit:

- Fleeing side faster: evasion succeeds automatically.
- Equal speed: **4-in-6 chance** of escape per day.
- Pursuers faster: **2-in-6 chance** of escape per day.

Apply ±1 for favorable or unfavorable terrain, weather, or visibility. A minimum **1-in-6 chance** always applies.

### Slowing Pursuit

A fleeing side may drop items to delay pursuers. Intelligent pursuers have a **3-in-6 chance** to pause for valuable treasure; unintelligent pursuers have a **3-in-6 chance** to stop for food. Each pause grants a fresh evasion attempt.

### Exhaustion

Sustained running has a cost. After **30 rounds** of dungeon pursuit or **a full day** of wilderness flight, all participants take **−1 to all rolls** until they rest for **3 turns** (dungeon) or a **full night** (wilderness). Mapping and tracking are impossible while running.
